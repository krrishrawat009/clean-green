Activity 1: Solar Photovoltaic System

System Architecture

Solar Radiation (Photons)
↓
Photovoltaic Panels (PV Cells)
↓
DC Electricity Generation
↓
Power Inverter (DC to AC Conversion)
↓
AC Electricity Distribution
↓
Grid Connection / Battery Storage / Load

System Explanation

Source of Energy: The primary energy source is solar radiation from the sun, which provides approximately 1000 watts per square meter of energy at peak conditions. This renewable energy source is abundant and freely available, making it an ideal candidate for sustainable power generation.

Conversion Process: Photovoltaic cells within solar panels utilize the photovoltaic effect to convert sunlight directly into electrical energy. When photons strike the semiconductor material (typically silicon), they excite electrons, creating electron-hole pairs. This generates direct current (DC) electricity. The DC output is then converted to alternating current (AC) through an inverter, making it compatible with standard electrical grids and household appliances.

Output and Utilization: The system produces clean electrical energy ranging from a few kilowatts for residential installations to megawatts for utility-scale solar farms. This electricity can power homes, businesses, and industrial facilities. Excess energy can be stored in battery systems or fed back into the electrical grid through net metering arrangements.

Real-world Application: Solar PV systems are increasingly deployed worldwide for residential rooftop installations, commercial buildings, and large-scale solar farms. They significantly reduce dependence on fossil fuels, lower electricity bills, and contribute to carbon emission reduction goals. Countries like India are rapidly expanding solar capacity to meet renewable energy targets and provide electricity access to remote areas.

Activity 2: Wind Energy Conversion System

System Architecture

Wind Kinetic Energy
↓
Wind Turbine Blades (Aerodynamic Capture)
↓
Mechanical Rotation (Shaft)
↓
Gearbox (Speed Multiplication)
↓
Electrical Generator
↓
Power Conditioning Unit
↓
Grid Connection / Energy Storage

System Explanation

Source of Energy: Wind energy originates from atmospheric air movement caused by uneven solar heating of Earth's surface, creating pressure differences. Wind represents a vast renewable energy resource with kinetic energy proportional to the cube of wind velocity, making high-wind locations particularly valuable for energy generation.

Conversion Process: Wind turbine blades are aerodynamically designed to capture wind's kinetic energy and convert it into rotational mechanical energy. The rotating shaft drives a gearbox that increases rotational speed to match the generator's requirements. The generator, typically a synchronous or asynchronous machine, converts this mechanical rotation into electrical energy through electromagnetic induction. Modern turbines use sophisticated control systems to optimize blade pitch and yaw orientation for maximum efficiency across varying wind conditions.

Output and Utilization: Modern wind turbines generate between 2-15 megawatts of electrical power depending on their size and wind conditions. This electricity is conditioned to match grid frequency and voltage specifications before distribution. Wind farms can collectively produce hundreds of megawatts, powering thousands of homes and contributing significantly to regional electricity grids.

Real-world Application: Wind energy systems are deployed extensively in both onshore and offshore installations globally. They provide clean, cost-effective electricity generation with minimal environmental impact. Wind farms in Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, and Rajasthan demonstrate India's commitment to renewable energy. Offshore wind farms, though more expensive, offer consistent high-speed winds and reduced visual impact, representing the future of wind energy expansion.

Activity 3: Hydroelectric Power Generation

System Architecture

Water Reservoir (Potential Energy)
↓
Penstock (Water Flow Channel)
↓
High-Velocity Water Jet (Kinetic Energy)
↓
Hydraulic Turbine (Francis/Kaplan/Pelton)
↓
Mechanical Shaft Rotation
↓
Synchronous Generator
↓
Transformer & Transmission System

System Explanation

Source of Energy: The primary energy source is water stored at elevation in natural or artificial reservoirs, possessing gravitational potential energy. This energy originates from the hydrological cycle driven by solar energy, which evaporates water, forms clouds, and creates precipitation that fills reservoirs, making hydroelectric power indirectly solar-powered and completely renewable.

Conversion Process: Water flows from the reservoir through penstocks, converting potential energy into kinetic energy as it accelerates. This high-velocity water strikes turbine blades, causing rotation and transferring kinetic energy to mechanical energy. The turbine type (Pelton for high heads, Francis for medium heads, Kaplan for low heads) is selected based on site-specific head and flow characteristics. The turbine shaft directly couples to a synchronous generator that produces electrical energy through electromagnetic induction at grid frequency.

Output and Utilization: Hydroelectric plants generate substantial power output ranging from small micro-hydro installations (less than 100 kW) to massive facilities exceeding 10,000 MW. The electricity generated is stepped up through transformers for long-distance transmission with minimal losses. Hydroelectric systems offer excellent load-following capability, making them ideal for peak demand management and grid stabilization alongside base load generation.

Real-world Application: Hydroelectric power represents one of the most mature and reliable renewable energy technologies, providing approximately 16% of global electricity. Major installations like the Three Gorges Dam in China and India's Bhakra Nangal Dam demonstrate the technology's scale and importance. Beyond electricity generation, hydroelectric facilities provide irrigation, flood control, and water supply benefits. Pumped storage hydroelectric systems enable large-scale energy storage, addressing the intermittency challenges of other renewable sources and enhancing overall grid reliability.
